Joint care — critical for you
Your joints clicking is a signal — not to stop training, but to condition them properly first. Follow every warmup exactly. Avoid locking out joints under load. Never train through sharp pain (dull muscle burn = fine, sharp joint pain = stop).
Avoid weeks 1–4
Heavy barbell squats, deadlifts, behind-neck press, dips with weight
Always do
Joint circles before every session · band pull-aparts · bodyweight primer
Supplement consider
Fish oil or flaxseed oil (₹200/month) · helps joint lubrication significantly
Weight jumps
Max 1–2 kg increase per week · never jump more than 2.5 kg at once

Main workout — 45–50 min
Week 1–4: Do 2 sets only at 50% effort. Week 5+: 3 sets. Week 9+: 4 sets. RPE = Rate of Perceived Exertion out of 10.
1
Flat dumbbell bench press
3×10–12RPE 6–790 secChest
▾
Primary chest builder. Lie flat, dumbbells at chest level, elbows at 45° (not flared wide — protects your shoulder joints). Press up, don't lock out elbows fully at top. Control the descent — 2 sec down.

Week 1–2 starting weight: 5–6 kg each dumbbell · Week 5+: add 1 kg when last set feels easy.
Tip: If your lower back arches off bench, plant feet flat on floor. Keep core tight. This is your joint protection — don't rush the weight up.
Avoid: Flaring elbows 90° outward — massive shoulder impingement risk at your current joint state.
2
Incline dumbbell press
3×10RPE 690 secUpper chest
▾
Set bench to 30–45°. Same technique as flat press but targets upper chest — crucial for a full, proportional chest at your height. Start 1–2 kg lighter than flat press.

Why important for you: At 184 cm, upper chest development gives the appearance of filling out your frame faster.
Tip: Don't go past 45° incline — turns it into a shoulder exercise and increases impingement risk.
3
Seated dumbbell shoulder press
3×10–12RPE 690 secShoulders
▾
Sit upright on bench with back support. Start with dumbbells at ear level, press up and slightly in — don't crash them together at top. Do NOT do behind-neck press — very bad for clicky joints.

Week 1–2 starting weight: 4–5 kg each.
Tip: Keep core braced throughout. Don't lean back excessively — if you are, the weight is too heavy.
Skip behind-neck press completely — dangerous for your current joint state.
4
Dumbbell lateral raises
3×12–15RPE 5–660 secSide delts
▾
Stand upright, slight bend in elbows, raise arms to shoulder height — no higher. Tilt dumbbells slightly forward (like pouring water). Control the descent.

This exercise is what makes shoulders look wide — especially important for filling out your 184 cm frame. Even tiny weights work here.
Tip: 3 kg is perfectly fine to start. Lateral delts are small muscles — ego check here. Slow and controlled beats heavy every time.
5
Tricep pushdowns (cable or dumbbell overhead)
3×12RPE 660 secTriceps
▾
If cable machine available: straight bar or rope pushdown · elbows locked at sides · push down fully, control up.
If no cable: Dumbbell overhead tricep extension — sit, hold one dumbbell with both hands behind head, extend up. Keep elbows pointing forward.

Starting weight: 5–8 kg cable / 6–8 kg dumbbell.
Tip: Triceps make up 2/3 of your arm size. This exercise pays off fast for arm appearance at your frame.
6
Dumbbell chest flyes (light)
2×12RPE 560 secChest stretch
▾
Lie flat, arms wide with slight elbow bend, lower dumbbells in arc until you feel a good chest stretch — bring back together. This is a stretch movement, not a strength one.

Keep very light: 4–5 kg max. Purpose is chest muscle lengthening and blood flow — doubles as cooldown prep.
Tip: Never go heavy on flyes with clicky joints — shoulder impingement risk is real. Light and slow is the rule.

1
Lat pulldowns (machine)
3×10–12RPE 6–790 secLats
▾
Wide grip, lean back slightly (10–15°), pull bar to upper chest. Don't pull behind neck. Squeeze lats at bottom, slow return — 2 sec up.

Why first: Lat pulldown teaches the back engagement pattern you need before rows. Your lats are likely very underdeveloped at 55 kg — this is your most important exercise right now.
Starting weight: 15–20 kg on stack.
Tip: Think "elbows to your back pockets" — this cue activates lats far better than just pulling down.
2
Seated cable row / dumbbell row
3×10RPE 690 secMid back
▾
If cable: sit tall, pull handle to belly button, squeeze shoulder blades at end, slow return. If dumbbell row: one hand + knee on bench, row dumbbell to hip — not to shoulder.

Keep back straight always — no rounding. At your current weight, rounding under load = injury risk.
Tip: Pause 1 sec at the contracted position (when elbows are back). This mind-muscle connection builds thickness faster.
3
Face pulls (cable or band)
3×15RPE 560 secRear delts + rotator cuff
▾
Cable at face height, pull rope toward face with elbows flared wide. This exercise is non-negotiable for your situation — it directly addresses the muscular imbalance causing shoulder clicking.

Think of this as joint therapy disguised as an exercise. Do this every single pull day.
Tip: If no cable, use a resistance band tied to a door handle. The movement matters more than the equipment.
This is the most important exercise for your clicking joints. Never skip it.
4
Dumbbell bicep curls
3×12RPE 660 secBiceps
▾
Stand tall, curl one or both dumbbells, elbows stay pinned at sides — don't swing. Supinate (rotate) wrist at top. 2 sec down.

Starting weight: 6–8 kg each. Don't ego lift here — swinging is useless and risks elbow tendon strain.
Tip: Do alternate arms if using heavy weight — gives each side more control and reduces swinging.
5
Hammer curls
3×12RPE 660 secBrachialis + forearm
▾
Same as bicep curl but thumbs point up (neutral grip) throughout. This targets brachialis — the muscle under your bicep that pushes it up, making arms look bigger overall. Also strengthens forearm and wrist — directly helps your clicky wrists.
Tip: Can be done seated to remove any tendency to sway. Control is everything here.
6
Assisted / negative pull-ups
2×5–8RPE 72 minFull back
▾
Week 1–4: Jump to top position, lower yourself as slowly as possible (5 sec down) — this is a negative pull-up. Builds strength without needing to do full reps.
Week 9+: Aim for full pull-ups once you can do 5 clean negatives.

At 55 kg your bodyweight is low — you may find these easier than expected. That's good — use it.
Tip: Grip slightly wider than shoulder width, pull elbows down to hips — not arms to bar. This distinction is everything for back activation.

Main workout — 45–50 min
1
Goblet squat (dumbbell)
3×10–12RPE 62 minQuads + glutes
▾
Hold one dumbbell at chest with both hands, squat down keeping chest up and knees tracking over toes. Go to parallel or slightly below.

Why goblet not barbell first: Goblet squat forces upright torso, teaches depth, and is much safer for your joints than barbell back squat. Progress to barbell in Phase 2–3.
Starting weight: 8–10 kg dumbbell.
Tip: Push knees out (don't let them cave in) — say "spread the floor with your feet" mentally. At 55 kg your knees will be under less stress than average — use this advantage.
2
Leg press (machine)
3×12RPE 6–72 minQuads + glutes
▾
Feet shoulder-width on platform, push through heels, don't lock out knees at top. Lower slowly — 3 sec down. This machine allows you to load legs safely without spine compression — perfect for your current state.

Starting weight: 30–40 kg on machine. Will feel light — that's correct in Phase 1.
Tip: Don't let lower back peel off the seat pad. If it does, your feet are too low or weight is too heavy.
Never lock out knees on leg press — joint stress is huge at lockout under load.
3
Romanian deadlift (dumbbell, light)
3×10RPE 690 secHamstrings + lower back
▾
Stand with dumbbells, hinge at hips (not squat), lower dumbbells along legs until you feel hamstring stretch, drive hips forward to come back up. Back stays neutral — not rounded.

Critical note: This is the most important posterior chain exercise for you. Weak hamstrings and lower back are common post-illness. Start at 8–10 kg each.

Do NOT do conventional deadlift from floor yet — too much lower back risk without a solid strength base.
Tip: Push your hips backward as you lower — not just bending forward. Feeling it in hamstrings = correct. Feeling it in lower back = too heavy or rounding.
4
Leg curl machine (or Nordic curl)
3×12RPE 660 secHamstrings
▾
If machine available: lie prone, curl heels to glutes, squeeze at top, slow return — 3 sec down. This isolates hamstrings without lower back involvement — safe and effective.

If no machine: Nordic curl — kneel on mat, partner/bench holds ankles, lower your body forward as slowly as possible using hamstrings.
Tip: Don't let hips lift off the pad during leg curls — this means the weight is too heavy.
5
Standing calf raises
4×20RPE 645 secCalves
▾
Stand on edge of step or flat floor, rise onto toes fully, lower all the way down (full range). Hold dumbbell in one hand for resistance once bodyweight feels easy.

Calves are slow-twitch dominant — they need high reps (20+) to grow. Don't expect results from 3×10 here.
Tip: Pause 1 sec at the top of each rep. This pause eliminates the bounce reflex and forces actual muscle contraction.
6
Plank + dead bug (core)
3 roundsRPE 560 secCore stability
▾
Plank: 30–45 sec · forearms on floor, body straight, don't let hips sag or rise · core tight throughout.
Dead bug (immediately after): Lie on back, arms up, legs up bent at 90° · lower opposite arm + leg toward floor without lower back lifting · 8 each side.

This combination builds the core strength needed to safely do squats and deadlifts with more weight later.
Tip: Core training here is investment — without a strong core, you can't safely add weight to compound lifts. This pays off in Phase 2–3.

▾
Start with dumbbells at shoulder height, palms facing you. As you press up, rotate palms outward so they face forward at the top. Reverse on the way down.

Hits all 3 heads of the deltoid in one movement — front, side, and rear. Excellent for building the "capped" shoulder look that makes a tall person like you look proportional.

Mewing — the foundation of everything
👅
Mewing — correct technique
24/7 habitJaw + palate + chinMost important
▾
What it is: Resting your entire tongue on the roof of your mouth (palate) while lips are sealed and teeth lightly touching. This is the correct human resting oral posture — most people have lost this due to mouth breathing.

Correct position:
1. Close your lips gently
2. Touch all your teeth together lightly (or slightly apart)
3. Place the TIP of tongue just behind front teeth
4. Now push the ENTIRE body of tongue flat against the entire roof of mouth
5. Breathe through your nose

What it changes at 20: Upward and forward pressure on palate → widens palate → more prominent cheekbones → better defined jaw → chin comes forward slightly · Takes 3–6 months for structural changes but jaw muscle tone improves in 4–6 weeks.

Common mistake: Only placing tongue tip. The entire tongue body must press up — this is what creates the palatal pressure.
Tip: Set a reminder every hour on your phone: "tongue up?" · Most people forget after 5 minutes at first. Within 3 weeks it becomes automatic.
If you feel jaw pain from mewing — you're clenching. The contact should be gentle, not forced. Mewing is a resting position, not a flex.
